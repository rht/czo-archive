Anders Kaseorg (May 19 2021 at 21:56):

Color management is complicated. Fancy displays have highly saturated primary colors that can be combined for a wider range of colors than normal, so web colors, which are specified in the sRGB colorspace, need to be converted to avoid becoming oversaturated. When you take a screenshot, the raw color values come from your display’s colorspace, so they should either be appropriately tagged with that colorspace, or converted back to sRGB, so they can later be displayed correctly. But some operating systems, browsers, screenshot applications, or tools you might use to edit your screenshots, skip those steps.

Anders Kaseorg (May 19 2021 at 22:04):

On Linux, where there’s typically no color management in any of these components unless you set it up manually, it’s hard to notice this problem. You might just assume that the unusually vivid colors coming out of your display are “correct” or even “better” if you notice them at all, and it only affects the light reaching your eyes, not the bits in your files. On Windows, or especially Mac, where Apple puts significant effort into getting color management right but third party applications are hit or miss, the discrepancy can be more noticeable.
